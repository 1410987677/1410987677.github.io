Pandemic-Era Theft in San Francisco: A Decade in Perspective¶

Understanding how theft-related crime evolved in San Francisco before and after the COVID-19 pandemic provides valuable insight into public safety, urban dynamics, and law enforcement patterns. This analysis uses open crime data from the San Francisco Police Department, covering incidents from 2003 to 2023.

In this short project, we focus specifically on theft-related crimes, examining how their frequency and distribution changed across time, neighborhoods, and even the time of day. By comparing trends pre- and post-COVID, we aim to reveal shifts in behavior and identify areas or time periods with notable changes.

The following visualizations break down the data in three ways: through a time-series view, spatial distribution on maps, and day/night crime patterns, concluding with an interactive visualization for deeper exploration.

Mapping Theft: Post-COVID Raw Location Distribution

The heatmap below visualizes the geographic distribution of theft incidents in San Francisco after the onset of the COVID-19 pandemic. Each area is color-coded by incident density, with warmer colors indicating higher concentrations of reported thefts.

We can observe that theft activity remains heavily concentrated in established urban hotspots such as Downtown, Mission, and Tenderloin. These areas have historically reported high theft rates, and they continue to do so in the post-pandemic period.

However, compared to pre-COVID trends (not shown here), there appears to be a slight dispersion of incidents into more residential and peripheral neighborhoods. The traditional urban core still dominates, but emerging pockets of activity hint at evolving patterns.

This geographic shift may reflect broader behavioral changes caused by the pandemic—remote work, reduced tourism, and fewer retail and nightlife activities in central areas may have redistributed routine movement patterns, thereby influencing where thefts occur.

Understanding these spatial shifts is critical for tailoring law enforcement resources, urban planning, and community safety initiatives in the post-pandemic city.

When Does Theft Happen? Day vs Night Trends¶

This grouped bar chart compares the number of thefts committed during the day (6am–6pm) versus at night (6pm–6am), across both time periods.

We observe that daytime thefts decreased sharply after COVID, more significantly than nighttime thefts. This pattern may reflect reduced daytime traffic in commercial areas—fewer commuters, tourists, or open businesses—resulting in fewer opportunities for theft.

Nighttime thefts also declined, but the drop is less dramatic. It's possible that changes in nightlife, policing, or residential patterns played a role. The pandemic disrupted daily routines, and this chart helps us understand how that translated into changes in crime timing.

Conclusion: A Changing Cityscape

Our analysis of theft-related crime in San Francisco reveals several key trends shaped by the COVID-19 pandemic, offering insight into how urban safety and criminal behavior have adapted during a period of unprecedented disruption:

Overall Decline in Theft: Reported theft incidents dropped significantly after 2020. This trend likely reflects pandemic-related factors such as lockdowns, business closures, decreased foot traffic, and shifts in public behavior—particularly in commercial districts.

Spatial Redistribution: While long-standing hotspots like Downtown, Mission, and Tenderloin remain active, the post-pandemic heatmap reveals signs of geographic diffusion. Theft activity shows subtle expansion into more residential and peripheral neighborhoods, indicating evolving spatial risk zones.

Shifts in Timing Patterns: The interactive day/night trend chart shows that thefts have decreased in both periods, but the drop is more pronounced during the day. This may be attributed to the widespread adoption of remote work and a decline in typical daytime routines that previously shaped urban crime opportunities.

These three visualizations—across time, space, and daily rhythm—together offer a multifaceted perspective on how theft patterns have shifted. While the pandemic clearly disrupted traditional crime dynamics, it's unclear whether these changes are temporary adaptations or the beginning of a long-term transformation in urban safety.

To truly understand these shifts, continued monitoring of post-pandemic data is necessary—alongside consideration of economic, social, and policy factors that influence crime.
