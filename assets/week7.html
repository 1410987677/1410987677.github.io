Pandemic-Era Theft in San Francisco: A Decade in Perspective

Understanding how theft-related crime evolved in San Francisco before and after the COVID-19 pandemic provides valuable insight into public safety, urban dynamics, and law enforcement patterns. This analysis uses open crime data from the San Francisco Police Department, covering incidents from 2003 to 2023.

In this short project, we focus specifically on theft-related crimes, examining how their frequency and distribution changed across time, neighborhoods, and even the time of day. By comparing trends pre- and post-COVID, we aim to reveal shifts in behavior and identify areas or time periods with notable changes.

The following visualizations break down the data in three ways: through a time-series view, spatial distribution on maps, and day/night crime patterns, concluding with an interactive visualization for deeper exploration.

Theft Over Time: A Two-Decade Trend

The line chart above illustrates the total number of theft-related incidents reported each year in San Francisco. The red dashed line marks the onset of the COVID-19 pandemic (March 2020).

From 2003 to around 2016, theft incidents were relatively stable with some annual fluctuations. A notable decline began around 2017, which sharply accelerated after 2020. The pandemic appears to coincide with a drop in theft casespossibly due to lockdowns, reduced mobility, and changes in law enforcement presence.

However, this trend may also reflect underreporting or data collection lags during the pandemic. It's important to consider both social behavior and data limitations when interpreting the decline.

Overall, this time-series plot offers a clear view of long-term trends and highlights how major events like the pandemic can reshape urban crime patterns.

Mapping Theft: Raw Location Distribution Before and After COVID

The static maps above show the geographic locations of theft incidents in San Francisco, split into pre- and post-COVID periods. Each red dot represents a reported theft case.

We can clearly observe that incidents are densely concentrated in urban centers, such as Downtown, Mission, and Tenderloin. These areas remain hotspots across both periods. However, after the pandemic began, there is a visible shift and slight dispersionfewer clusters are observed in central areas, and more dots appear in residential zones.

This change might reflect pandemic-related shifts in human behavior: reduced tourism, remote work, and the temporary closure of retail or nightlife spots. While the core pattern of theft-prone areas remains stable, the density and shape of activity seem to have adapted to the new social landscape.

Interactive Heatmaps: Theft Density Across the City

These interactive heatmaps show the geographic intensity of theft reports in San Francisco before and after COVID. Brighter regions indicate higher concentrations of incidents.

Both maps highlight key hotspots in neighborhoods like Downtown, Mission, and parts of the Bayview area. However, the Post-COVID map shows a slightly lower overall intensity, and the density appears more distributed across the city.

These changes could suggest shifting crime opportunities as human movement, business activity, and police focus all evolved during the pandemic.

When Does Theft Happen? Day vs Night Trends

This grouped bar chart compares the number of thefts committed during the day (6am6pm) versus at night (6pm6am), across both time periods.

We observe that daytime thefts decreased sharply after COVID, more significantly than nighttime thefts. This pattern may reflect reduced daytime traffic in commercial areasfewer commuters, tourists, or open businessesresulting in fewer opportunities for theft.

Nighttime thefts also declined, but the drop is less dramatic. It's possible that changes in nightlife, policing, or residential patterns played a role. The pandemic disrupted daily routines, and this chart helps us understand how that translated into changes in crime timing.

Conclusion: A Changing Cityscape

Our analysis of theft-related crime in San Francisco reveals several key trends shaped by the COVID-19 pandemic:

  • Overall Decline in Theft: Theft incidents dropped noticeably after 2020, likely due to lockdowns, fewer people in public spaces, and changes in daily routines.
  • Spatial Redistribution: Traditional hotspots like Downtown and the Mission District remain active, but theres evidence of slight dispersal into other areas post-pandemic.
  • Shifts in Timing: Both daytime and nighttime thefts decreased, but daytime incidents declined more dramaticallypossibly due to remote work and fewer daytime targets.

While the pandemic clearly disrupted crime patterns, its still unclear whether these shifts are temporary adaptations or signs of lasting transformation in urban crime dynamics. Ongoing monitoring, along with socio-economic and policy context, is essential for deeper understanding.

This project demonstrates the power of open data and narrative visualization to make complex patterns accessible and actionable. Whether youre a policymaker, city planner, or curious citizen, visual data stories can help you understand the city you live inand how its changing.
